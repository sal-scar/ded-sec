Immigration & Crime: What the Evidence Actually Says

Crime statistics are easy to weaponize. This guide explains the research patterns and how to avoid misleading comparisons.

Updated: 2026-01-11 • Evidence-first article

Why you can trust this guide

It emphasizes reputable research and careful interpretation (age structure, reporting bias, and definitions), not blame narratives.

1) Start with definitions (they change everything)

“Immigrant”, “asylum seeker”, “undocumented”, and “foreign-born” are not the same category. Many datasets mix or separate them differently.

Crime data also varies: arrests, convictions, victimization surveys, and police reports measure different things.

2) The biggest confounder: age and sex

Crime rates correlate strongly with age and sex (young males commit more offences on average across populations).

So comparing two groups without adjusting for age/sex structure can be misleading.

Why this matters

A country that receives many young workers will look ‘riskier’ on raw rates even if behavior is the same.

3) What research often finds

Across many studies, the relationship between immigration and crime is mixed and often small; in some contexts immigration correlates with lower crime, in others there’s little effect. It depends on integration, labor markets, and local conditions.

Using “one country has immigrants / one doesn’t” comparisons is rarely valid—countries differ in reporting, policing, demographics, and inequality.

4) How to read statistics responsibly

Prefer peer-reviewed research and national statistical agencies. Check whether a chart uses per-capita rates, adjusted models, and consistent definitions over time.

Be skeptical of viral screenshots and cherry-picked examples. Ask: what’s the denominator, and what’s missing?

  • Per 100,000 population (not raw counts)
  • Age/sex adjustment
  • Compare similar regions
  • Look at trends, not one year

5) Productive policy questions (without scapegoating)

If you care about safety, focus on what works: policing quality, community trust, legal pathways, fast processing, and integration into work and education.
